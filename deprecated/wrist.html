Wrist Conduct.

WristConduct is a project I worked on in winter 2022 with two fellow students. We summarized our work in a paper WristConduct: Biometric User Authentication Using Bone Conduction at the Wrist that you can find below.

As the title of the paper suggests the project was on authenticating users by their wrist. To do that we used a bone conduction speaker to send a three-second white noise sound signal through the tests subjects wrist and a larynx microphone to record the signal at the other side of the wrist. Using machine learning we trained models to authenticate a test subject.

Download Paper

Study Setup

The study was conducted at Frankfurt University of Applied Science. We recorded 10 - 12 recordings of 24 participants.

The images below show our apparatus in action. The silver cylinder is the bone conduction speaker. The black device on the upper side of the wrist is the larynx microphone. A larynx microphone was used since it does not pick up any surrounding sound that travel through the air, but only the sound waves that come through the wrist.

Model Training

Since we were intrusted in authentication, we built a binary classifier that is trained on one test subject. The classifier either accepts (output 1) or rejects (output 2) a new recording.
Since we had 24 participants we created 24 datasets were in each dataset one the recordings of one test subject were the positive samples and all other recordings were labeled as negatives.
These datasets are highly unbalanced with about 10 positive to 230 negative samples. So what we did is create 5 datasets for each test subject that hold all positive samples for that subject and 92 shuffled negative samples.
Therefore we trained 5 models for each 24 test subjects. As a model we first used a SDG Support Vector Machine with good results. We then used a deep neural network to achieve even better results.

Results

These are the results of the neural networks. The Metrics are the mean for all (5 times 24) models that were trained.
